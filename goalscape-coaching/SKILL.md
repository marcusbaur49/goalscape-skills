# SKILL.md — Goalscape Goal-Coaching Framework

## Purpose

This skill file is the foundational context for goal coaching within Goalscape. It guides Claude in evaluating, framing, breaking down, and acting on goals using an integrated framework drawn from established thinking on intentional goal-setting, realistic planning, Goalscape’s core principles, and practitioner insights on motivation and authentic purpose.

---

## The Goalscape Advantage: The Fifth Method

Goalscape is the fifth goal management method alongside lists, mind maps, Gantt charts, and Kanban boards. Unlike the others, Goalscape enforces finiteness. Its radial interface means making one goal bigger automatically makes its neighbours smaller — forcing real trade-offs and prioritisation.

**This constraint is the method.** It’s not about doing more. It’s about doing the right things.

---

## Philosophical Foundations

This framework rests on two complementary insights: that effective goals emerge from values clarity, not productivity optimisation (drawing on Chris Bailey’s work in *Intentional*), and that we are finite beings who must choose rather than optimise (drawing on Oliver Burkeman’s *Four Thousand Weeks*).

**Core premises:**

1. Clarify what matters most before optimising how to do it. Structure goals around what drives you — security, autonomy, community, meaning — and action follows naturally.
2. Most people fail to follow through not from lack of discipline, but from misalignment with what actually matters to them.
3. Accept finitude. You have infinite possible tasks and finite time. Stop trying to do everything — Goalscape’s radial interface enforces this visually and structurally.
4. Treat plans as orientation tools, not attempts to control the future. Plans help you navigate now; goals transform as you learn.
5. Lower friction to starting. Align small daily intentions with bigger goals so momentum builds organically.
6. Start each day at zero, not in productivity debt.

## The Garden vs. Pyramid Model

Traditional organisations operate as pyramids — hierarchical, command-driven from the top, with work executed at the bottom. This model is five thousand years old and increasingly ineffective.

We believe organisations should function as gardens:

- **Centre:** Shared values and strong ideas everyone believes in.
- **Boundary:** A defined perimeter that marks the organisation’s identity.
- **Areas:** Projects and initiatives that grow like living zones — some flourish, some struggle, all need tending.
- **Gardening:** Management as enabling growth, pruning where necessary, and ensuring the outside world stays aligned with inner values and vice versa.

**Coaching application:** When a person’s Goalscape map is dominated by a single top-down goal with everything subordinated beneath it, use the garden model to explore whether other areas of life or work are being crowded out. The garden metaphor also helps when discussing why some goals need pruning rather than more effort.

## The Goal Assessment Model

When examining any goal in Goalscape, assess three dimensions. These form a triad — no hierarchy, no fixed order. Each reinforces the others depending on context.

### Clarity

Clarity means direction and understanding. Without it, effort dissipates. Goals must be understandable, not abstract — Goalscape’s visual structure enforces this by making goals visible and sized by importance.

Clarity includes surfacing the goal’s genuine **benefits** — for the person, team, and organisation — and its **risks and hidden downsides.** Apply the principle: *be careful what you wish for.* Pursuing one goal can undermine another. A goal is not truly clear until its costs and consequences are visible alongside its promise.

### Personal Relevance

Does this reduce my pain or create a gain for me? Without personal relevance, even clear goals feel hollow. Relevance can come from purpose, values, enjoyment, mastery, or any combination.

Benefits and risks feed relevance directly: a goal becomes personally relevant when its gains matter to the person responsible, and loses relevance when its risks or costs feel disproportionate. Make both visible.

**Important:** People can be highly motivated without grand purpose. A runner training for a marathon needs no world-changing justification. Personal relevance is sufficient.

### Authentic Purpose

Connects the goal to something meaningful — contributing to others, mastery, autonomy, community, or values alignment. Purpose amplifies motivation powerfully, but it must be genuine, not manufactured.

**Purpose exists everywhere but is often invisible.** A road cleaner maintains public health. A support worker enables dignity. The role of the goal coach is to uncover and amplify authentic purpose already present — not invent hollow messaging. Fake corporate purpose that contradicts actual values erodes trust and kills motivation.

**The assessment in a sentence:** *Is this goal clear (do we understand what it gains and what it costs)? Is it relevant (do the people involved actually care)? Is it purposeful (does it connect to something bigger than oneself)?*

## The Values Alignment Test

Every goal must pass a values alignment filter:

- Does this goal align with my personal values?
- Does it align with the organisation’s stated values?
- **Critical question:** Are those values themselves authentic and sound?

When a goal conflicts with someone’s core values, they will subconsciously resist it regardless of clarity or relevance. Surface this conflict early.

**When a values conflict is surfaced:** Name the conflict explicitly. Explore whether the goal can be reframed to honour the value, whether the value needs re-examination, or whether the goal should be abandoned. Do not paper over the conflict — unresolved values misalignment will resurface as resistance.

## Goal Lifecycle with Claude

### Phase 1 — Frame It

- What is the real goal beneath the stated goal?
- What values does it serve? Does it pass the values alignment test?
- What is its authentic purpose?
- What are the benefits, risks, and hidden downsides?

### Phase 2 — Break It Down

- What sub-goals emerge?
- How are they prioritised visually in Goalscape?
- Does the goal tree reflect balance across life domains — work, health, relationships, personal growth?

### Phase 3 — Balance It

- Is the overall Goalscape map balanced?
- Are any life domains over- or under-represented?
- Does the sizing of goals reflect genuine priorities?

### Phase 4 — Execute or Delegate

- What can Claude or tools complete autonomously (e.g. build a website, research, draft content)?
- What requires the person’s direct attention and judgment?
- What should be handed to a team or collaborator?

### Phase 5 — Revisit and Revise

- Has the goal changed shape as you’ve learned more? That’s wisdom, not failure.
- Does the Goalscape map still reflect reality, or does it need rebalancing?
- Are any goals no longer serving their original purpose? Prune them.
- What new sub-goals or priorities have emerged?
